Alkenes & Alkynes: Fundamental Concepts & Reaction Types

What are Alkenes and Alkynes?

Alkenes are hydrocarbons containing carbon-carbon double bonds ($C=C$). Alkynes contain carbon-carbon triple bonds ($C≡C$). These unsaturated hydrocarbons are highly reactive due to the presence of π-bonds which are weaker than σ-bonds.

Key Concept: Markovnikov's Rule & Anti-Markovnikov

Markovnikov's Rule: "The rich get richer." In addition of unsymmetrical reagents to unsymmetrical alkenes, the positive part adds to carbon with more hydrogens. Anti-Markovnikov: Occurs with peroxides (HBr only) or via hydroboration-oxidation.

MDCAT/NUMS Focus Areas:

  • Major and minor product determination
  • Reaction mechanisms (electrophilic addition)
  • Stereochemistry (cis/trans, syn/anti addition)
  • Reaction conditions and catalysts
  • Distinguishing tests between alkene and alkane

Competition Level Exceptional Cases:

  1. Peroxide effect: Only with HBr, not HCl or HI
  2. Hydroboration-oxidation: Anti-Markovnikov, syn addition
  3. Oxidation with cold vs hot KMnO₄: Different products
  4. Stereospecific reactions: Syn addition in hydrogenation, anti in halogenation
MCQ Solving Trick: Product Prediction Mnemonic

Mnemonic: "HAPPY" - Hydrogen adds to Poor carbon (less substituted) and Positive part adds to carbon with more Hydrogens (Markovnikov). For anti-Markovnikov with HBr/peroxide: "BRO" - Bromine adds to Rich carbon (more substituted).

General Reaction: $R-CH=CH₂ + X-Y → R-CHX-CH₂Y$ (Electrophilic Addition)

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Alkene + H₂ (Pt/Pd/Ni) → Alkane (syn addition)
  2. Alkene + HX → Alkyl halide (Markovnikov product)
  3. Alkene + H₂O/H⁺ → Alcohol (Markovnikov, acid-catalyzed)
  4. Alkene + X₂ (Cl₂, Br₂) → Vicinal dihalide (anti addition)
  5. Alkene + X₂/H₂O → Halohydrin (Markovnikov, anti addition)

Detailed MCQ Explanation Example:

Sample MCQ with Detailed Analysis

Question: Propene reacts with HBr in presence of peroxide. The major product is:

Options: A) 1-Bromopropane, B) 2-Bromopropane, C) 1,2-Dibromopropane, D) No reaction

Step-by-step solution:

  1. Normal addition of HBr to propene gives 2-bromopropane (Markovnikov)
  2. But with peroxide, anti-Markovnikov addition occurs (only for HBr)
  3. In anti-Markovnikov, Br adds to less substituted carbon
  4. Propene: CH₃-CH=CH₂ + HBr → CH₃-CH₂-CH₂Br (1-bromopropane)
  5. Thus answer is 1-bromopropane → Option A

Learning Trick: Remember "Peroxide changes HBr behavior ONLY". HCl and HI don't show peroxide effect.

Conceptual Understanding (5/30)

Why are alkenes more reactive than alkanes?
Answer: Alkenes have π-bonds which are weaker than σ-bonds. The π-electron cloud is exposed and can be easily attacked by electrophiles. Additionally, the double bond creates an area of high electron density that attracts electrophiles, making addition reactions favorable.
What determines major and minor products in alkene reactions?
Answer: The stability of carbocation intermediate. More substituted carbocations (3° > 2° > 1°) are more stable. Markovnikov's rule predicts the major product based on carbocation stability. Steric factors and reaction conditions can also influence product distribution.
Why does HBr show peroxide effect but HCl and HI do not?
Answer: HBr has the right bond dissociation energy for peroxide-initiated radical chain reaction. H-Cl bond is too strong to be broken by peroxide radicals, while H-I bond is too weak and iodine radicals combine instead of adding to alkene. This makes the peroxide effect specific to HBr.
What is the difference between syn and anti addition?
Answer: Syn addition: Both atoms add to the same face of the double bond (e.g., hydrogenation with Pt/Pd). Anti addition: Atoms add to opposite faces (e.g., bromination of alkenes). Syn gives erythro products (similar groups on same side), anti gives threo (similar groups on opposite sides) for disubstituted alkenes.
How can you distinguish between alkane and alkene in the lab?
Answer: Three common tests: 1) Bromine water test: Alkenes decolorize brown Br₂ (addition reaction), alkanes do not. 2) Bayer's test: Cold alkaline KMnO₄ turns from purple to colorless/brown with alkenes (oxidation). 3) Ozonolysis: Alkenes produce carbonyl compounds detected by specific tests.

Hydrogenation of Alkenes & Alkynes

Hydrogenation Definition:

Hydrogenation is the addition of hydrogen ($H_2$) across a double or triple bond in presence of a catalyst. For alkenes, it gives alkanes; for alkynes, it can give alkenes (partial) or alkanes (complete).

$R-CH=CH-R' + H_2 \xrightarrow[\text{catalyst}]{} R-CH_2-CH_2-R'$
Key Concept: Catalysts & Syn Addition

Common catalysts: Pt, Pd, Ni (heterogeneous) or Wilkinson's catalyst (homogeneous). Hydrogenation proceeds via syn addition - both H atoms add to the same face. This is stereospecific and gives meso compounds from symmetrical cis-alkenes and racemic mixtures from trans-alkenes.

Reaction Conditions

Catalysts: Pt, Pd, Ni (finely divided), Rh, Ru
Temperature: Room temperature to 150°C
Pressure: 1-5 atm (higher for less reactive alkenes)
Solvent: Usually non-polar (hexane, cyclohexane) or acetic acid

Mechanism Steps:

Step 1: Adsorption

Hydrogen and alkene adsorb onto metal surface. H-H bond breaks heterolytically to give metal-H bonds.

Step 2: Syn Addition

Hydrogen atoms transfer one by one to the same face of alkene, giving alkane.

Exceptional Case: Lindlar's Catalyst

For alkynes: $Pd/CaCO_3$ poisoned with quinoline or lead acetate gives cis-alkene (syn addition stops at alkene stage). For trans-alkene from alkyne: use Na/NH₃(l) (birch reduction, via radical anion).

Learning Mnemonic

"P-P-N for H-H on C=C" - Pt, Pd, Ni catalysts for Hydrogen addition on double bond. "Lindlar for cis, Na/NH₃ for trans" - Remember alkyne partial hydrogenation products.

MDCAT Problem Solving Strategy:

  1. Identify if substrate is alkene or alkyne
  2. Check catalyst: Normal (Pt/Pd/Ni) gives alkane; Lindlar gives cis-alkene; Na/NH₃ gives trans-alkene
  3. For stereochemistry: Syn addition means same side addition
  4. Heat of hydrogenation indicates stability: less heat → more stable alkene

Detailed MCQ Explanation Example:

Sample MCQ with Detailed Analysis

Question: 2-Butyne on hydrogenation with Lindlar's catalyst gives:

Options: A) cis-2-Butene, B) trans-2-Butene, C) Butane, D) 1-Butene

Step-by-step solution:

  1. Lindlar's catalyst: Pd/CaCO₃ poisoned with quinoline
  2. It gives syn addition to alkyne, stopping at alkene stage
  3. Syn addition to internal alkyne gives cis-alkene
  4. 2-Butyne: CH₃-C≡C-CH₃ + H₂ (Lindlar) → CH₃-CH=CH-CH₃ (cis)
  5. Thus answer is cis-2-butene → Option A

Learning Trick: "Lindlar gives cis, Na/NH₃ gives trans" for alkyne partial hydrogenation.

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Hydrogenation is exothermic (heat released)
  2. Rate: terminal alkene > internal alkene > alkane (already saturated)
  3. Heat of hydrogenation indicates stability: lower heat → more stable
  4. Catalyst reduces activation energy, not thermodynamic parameters
  5. Syn addition gives meso from symmetrical cis, racemic from trans

Conceptual Understanding (10/30)

Why is hydrogenation syn addition?
Answer: Because both hydrogen atoms add from the same side of the metal surface. The alkene and hydrogen adsorb on catalyst surface, and hydrogens transfer sequentially while alkene remains attached to surface, forcing addition to same face. This is surface catalysis mechanism.
How does heat of hydrogenation relate to alkene stability?
Answer: Heat of hydrogenation is inversely proportional to stability. More stable alkenes release less heat upon hydrogenation. Order of stability: tetrasubstituted > trisubstituted > disubstituted > monosubstituted > unsubstituted. Cycloalkenes: cyclopentene > cyclohexene > cyclobutene > cyclopropene (most strained).
Why is Lindlar's catalyst "poisoned"?
Answer: Poisoning with quinoline or lead acetate reduces catalyst activity, allowing hydrogenation to stop at alkene stage instead of going to alkane. Without poisoning, Pd catalyst is too active and gives complete hydrogenation to alkane. The poison blocks some active sites.

Hydrohalogenation of Alkenes & Alkynes

Hydrohalogenation Definition:

Addition of hydrogen halides (HCl, HBr, HI) to alkenes and alkynes. For alkenes: gives alkyl halides. For alkynes: can give geminal dihalides (two halogens on same carbon) via Markovnikov addition.

$R-CH=CH_2 + HX \rightarrow R-CHX-CH_3$ (Markovnikov product)
Key Concept: Markovnikov vs Anti-Markovnikov

Markovnikov: H adds to less substituted carbon, X to more substituted (normal condition). Anti-Markovnikov: Only with HBr in presence of peroxides (ROOR) or via radical mechanism. HCl and HI don't show peroxide effect.

Reaction Conditions

Reagents: HCl, HBr, HI (gas or concentrated solution)
Solvent: Usually acetic acid or no solvent (neat)
Peroxide effect: Only for HBr with ROOR (benzoyl peroxide)
Temperature: Room temperature or below (to prevent polymerization)

Mechanism Steps (Electrophilic Addition):

Step 1: Formation of Carbocation

Proton (H⁺) attacks π-bond, forming more stable carbocation (Markovnikov). For unsymmetrical alkenes, proton adds to less substituted carbon to give more stable carbocation.

Step 2: Nucleophilic Attack

Halide ion (X⁻) attacks carbocation, giving alkyl halide. This step is fast.

Exceptional Case: Peroxide Effect Mechanism

With peroxides, HBr adds via radical mechanism: ROOR → 2RO• (initiation); RO• + HBr → ROH + Br•; Br• adds to alkene giving radical intermediate; radical abstracts H from HBr. This gives anti-Markovnikov product.

Learning Mnemonic

"M-A-R-K" - Markovnikov: Add to Rich Carbon (more substituted). "P-E-B" - Peroxide Effect for HBr only. "H-B-R" - HCl and HI don't show peroxide effect.

MDCAT Problem Solving Strategy:

  1. Check if peroxide is mentioned: If yes and reagent is HBr → anti-Markovnikov
  2. For unsymmetrical alkene: determine more/less substituted carbons
  3. For alkynes: Markovnikov addition gives geminal dihalide (two X on same C)
  4. Reactivity order: HI > HBr > HCl (based on bond strength)

Detailed MCQ Explanation Example:

Sample MCQ with Detailed Analysis

Question: 2-Methylpropene reacts with HCl. The product is:

Options: A) 1-Chloro-2-methylpropane, B) 2-Chloro-2-methylpropane, C) 1,2-Dichloro-2-methylpropane, D) No reaction

Step-by-step solution:

  1. 2-Methylpropene: (CH₃)₂C=CH₂ (isobutylene)
  2. Markovnikov addition: H adds to CH₂ (less substituted)
  3. Forms tertiary carbocation: (CH₃)₂C⁺-CH₃ (most stable)
  4. Cl⁻ attacks giving 2-chloro-2-methylpropane
  5. Thus answer is 2-chloro-2-methylpropane → Option B

Learning Trick: For alkene with tertiary carbon at double bond, product is tertiary halide (most stable carbocation).

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Markovnikov product is major due to carbocation stability
  2. Reactivity: HI > HBr > HCl (weaker H-X bond more reactive)
  3. Peroxide effect: Only for HBr, gives anti-Markovnikov
  4. With alkynes: Markovnikov gives geminal dihalides
  5. Carbocation stability: 3° > 2° > 1° > methyl

Hydration of Alkenes & Alkynes

Hydration Definition:

Addition of water ($H_2O$) across double or triple bond. For alkenes: gives alcohols (Markovnikov). For alkynes: gives carbonyl compounds (ketones or aldehydes) via enol intermediate that tautomerizes.

$R-CH=CH_2 + H_2O \xrightarrow[H^+]{H^+} R-CH(OH)-CH_3$ (Markovnikov alcohol)
Key Concept: Acid-Catalyzed vs Oxymercuration-Demercuration

Acid-catalyzed: $H_2SO_4$ or $H_3PO_4$, gives Markovnikov alcohol, carbocation rearrangements possible. Oxymercuration-demercuration: $Hg(OAc)_2/H_2O$ then $NaBH_4$, Markovnikov without rearrangement. Hydroboration-oxidation: $BH_3$ then $H_2O_2/OH^-$, anti-Markovnikov, syn addition.

Different Hydration Methods

1. Acid-catalyzed: $H_2SO_4$ (conc.), heat, Markovnikov, rearrangements
2. Oxymercuration-demercuration: $Hg(OAc)_2/H_2O$ then $NaBH_4$, Markovnikov, no rearrangement
3. Hydroboration-oxidation: $BH_3$ then $H_2O_2/OH^-$, anti-Markovnikov, syn addition
For alkynes: $HgSO_4/H_2SO_4/H_2O$ gives methyl ketones (Markovnikov)

Mechanism Steps (Acid-Catalyzed):

Step 1: Protonation

Proton attacks alkene forming carbocation (Markovnikov). Rearrangement possible if more stable carbocation can form.

Step 2: Nucleophilic Attack

Water attacks carbocation forming oxonium ion.

Step 3: Deprotonation

Loss of proton gives alcohol.

Exceptional Case: Hydroboration-Oxidation

Anti-Markovnikov, syn addition. Borane ($BH_3$) adds to alkene (H to less substituted, B to more substituted). Oxidation with $H_2O_2/OH^-$ replaces B with OH, retaining configuration. No carbocation, so no rearrangements.

Learning Mnemonic

"A-O-H" - Acid = Markovnikov with rearrangements; Oxymercuration = Markovnikov without rearrangements; Hydroboration = anti-Markovnikov, syn addition. "K-A" - For alkynes: Ketone from internal, Aldehyde from terminal (with $Hg^{2+}$).

Detailed MCQ Explanation Example:

Sample MCQ with Detailed Analysis

Question: 3,3-Dimethyl-1-butene undergoes acid-catalyzed hydration. The major product is:

Options: A) 3,3-Dimethyl-1-butanol, B) 3,3-Dimethyl-2-butanol, C) 2,3-Dimethyl-2-butanol, D) 2,3-Dimethyl-3-butanol

Step-by-step solution:

  1. Alkene: (CH₃)₃C-CH=CH₂
  2. Normal Markovnikov: H to CH₂, OH to tertiary carbon gives 3,3-dimethyl-2-butanol
  3. But initial carbocation: (CH₃)₃C-CH⁺-CH₃ (secondary)
  4. Rearrangement: methyl shift gives (CH₃)₂C⁺-CH(CH₃)₂ (tertiary, more stable)
  5. Water attacks giving 2,3-dimethyl-2-butanol → Option C

Learning Trick: Always check for possible carbocation rearrangements (hydride or alkyl shifts) when adjacent to tertiary carbon or with strained rings.

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Acid-catalyzed: Markovnikov, possible rearrangements
  2. Oxymercuration: Markovnikov, no rearrangements
  3. Hydroboration: anti-Markovnikov, syn addition
  4. For alkynes: $Hg^{2+}$ gives methyl ketone except terminal alkyne gives aldehyde
  5. Hydroboration-oxidation of terminal alkyne gives aldehyde

Halogenation of Alkenes & Alkynes

Halogenation Definition:

Addition of halogens ($X_2$ where X = Cl, Br, I) across double or triple bonds. For alkenes: gives vicinal dihalides (anti addition). For alkynes: can give tetrahalides or dihaloalkenes depending on conditions.

$R-CH=CH-R' + X_2 \rightarrow R-CHX-CHX-R'$ (Vicinal dihalide, anti addition)
Key Concept: Anti Addition via Halonium Ion

Halogenation proceeds via cyclic halonium ion intermediate, leading to anti addition. The halogen attacks from opposite side of halonium ion. Bromine water test for unsaturation: Br₂ (brown) decolorizes with alkene/alkyne.

Reaction Conditions

Reagents: $Cl_2$, $Br_2$, $I_2$ (Br₂ most common in lab)
Solvent: $CCl_4$, $CHCl_3$, $CH_2Cl_2$ (inert, non-nucleophilic)
For alkynes: Excess halogen gives tetrahalide, 1 equivalent gives trans-dihaloalkene
Stereochemistry: Anti addition via halonium ion

Mechanism Steps (for Br₂):

Step 1: Formation of Bromonium Ion

Br₂ approaches π-bond, polarization occurs. One Br attacks forming cyclic bromonium ion (3-membered ring) and Br⁻ leaves.

Step 2: Backside Attack

Br⁻ attacks from opposite side (backside attack) opening bromonium ion. This gives vicinal dibromide with anti stereochemistry.

Exceptional Case: Stereochemistry with Cyclic Alkenes

With cyclohexene: anti addition gives racemic mixture of dl pair (both trans). With cyclopentene or smaller rings: anti addition gives meso compound if symmetrical. Bromonium ion explains stereospecificity.

Learning Mnemonic

"B-A-D" - Bromination is Anti addition via cyclic halonium ion. "T-T-E" - Terminal alkyne with 1 eq Br₂ gives trans-dibromoalkene; with excess gives tetrahalide.

Detailed MCQ Explanation Example:

Sample MCQ with Detailed Analysis

Question: Cyclohexene reacts with Br₂ in CCl₄. The product is:

Options: A) cis-1,2-Dibromocyclohexane, B) trans-1,2-Dibromocyclohexane, C) 1-Bromocyclohexane, D) 3-Bromocyclohexene

Step-by-step solution:

  1. Br₂ adds to cyclohexene via bromonium ion
  2. Anti addition: Br⁻ attacks from opposite side
  3. In cyclohexane chair, both bromines end up trans-diaxial or trans-diequatorial
  4. Product is trans-1,2-dibromocyclohexane (racemic mixture)
  5. Thus answer is trans-1,2-dibromocyclohexane → Option B

Learning Trick: For cyclic alkenes, bromination always gives trans product (anti addition).

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Anti addition via cyclic halonium ion
  2. Reactivity: $Br_2 > Cl_2 > I_2$ (I₂ often reversible)
  3. Bromine water test: decolorization indicates unsaturation
  4. With alkynes: 1 eq gives trans-dihaloalkene, excess gives tetrahalide
  5. Stereospecific: cis-alkene gives meso (if symmetrical) or dl pair

Halohydrin Formation (Halo Hydration)

Halohydrin Formation Definition:

Addition of halogen and hydroxyl group ($X-OH$) across double bond. Reaction of alkene with halogen ($X_2$) in water or aqueous solvent gives halohydrin. Markovnikov orientation: OH goes to more substituted carbon, X to less substituted.

$R-CH=CH_2 + Br_2 + H_2O \rightarrow R-CHBr-CH_2OH$ (Markovnikov, anti addition)
Key Concept: Regioselectivity & Stereochemistry

Halohydrin formation shows Markovnikov regioselectivity (OH to more substituted carbon) due to carbocation-like character in halonium ion opening. It shows anti stereochemistry (like halogenation) because water attacks halonium ion from opposite side.

Reaction Conditions

Reagents: $X_2$ (Br₂ or Cl₂) in $H_2O$ or aqueous solvent
Solvent: Water, $CH_3COOH/H_2O$ mixture
Orientation: Markovnikov (OH to more substituted carbon)
Stereochemistry: Anti addition (OH and X trans to each other)

Mechanism Steps:

Step 1: Formation of Halonium Ion

Same as halogenation: X⁺ attacks alkene forming cyclic halonium ion and X⁻.

Step 2: Water Attack (Regioselective)

Water (nucleophile) attacks more substituted carbon of halonium ion (Markovnikov). This is because the positive charge is more on more substituted carbon (carbocation character).

Step 3: Deprotonation

Loss of proton gives halohydrin. Water acts as base.

Exceptional Case: Intramolecular Halohydrin Formation

If alkene has OH group nearby, intramolecular attack can give cyclic ethers (epoxides or larger rings). This is faster than intermolecular attack by water and gives specific stereochemistry.

Learning Mnemonic

"W-A-T" - Water Attacks halonium ion at more substituted carbon (like carbocation). "O-H to Rich, X to Poor" - OH goes to more substituted, X to less substituted carbon.

Detailed MCQ Explanation Example:

Sample MCQ with Detailed Analysis

Question: Styrene (C₆H₅-CH=CH₂) reacts with Br₂ in water. The major product is:

Options: A) C₆H₅-CHBr-CH₂Br, B) C₆H₅-CHBr-CH₂OH, C) C₆H₅-CH(OH)-CH₂Br, D) C₆H₅-CH(OH)-CH₂OH

Step-by-step solution:

  1. Halohydrin formation: Br₂ + H₂O with alkene
  2. Forms bromonium ion intermediate
  3. Water attacks more substituted carbon (benzyl carbon stabilizes positive charge)
  4. OH goes to benzyl carbon (more substituted), Br to CH₂
  5. Product: C₆H₅-CH(OH)-CH₂Br → Option C

Learning Trick: For conjugated alkenes (with phenyl, vinyl, etc.), positive charge stabilizes at benzylic position, so OH goes there.

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Markovnikov orientation: OH to more substituted carbon
  2. Anti stereochemistry: OH and X trans in open chain
  3. Nucleophile can be varied: ROH gives alkoxy halides
  4. With unsymmetrical alkenes: regioselectivity follows carbocation stability
  5. Bromine preferred over chlorine (more reactive, better halonium ion)

Epoxidation of Alkenes

Epoxidation Definition:

Formation of epoxides (oxiranes) from alkenes using peroxy acids or other oxidants. Epoxides are 3-membered cyclic ethers, highly strained and reactive. Common reagent: m-chloroperbenzoic acid (MCPBA).

$R-CH=CH-R' + RCO_3H \rightarrow R-CH-CH-R' + RCO_2H$ (Epoxide + carboxylic acid)
Key Concept: Stereospecific Syn Addition

Epoxidation is stereospecific syn addition. Configuration of alkene is retained in epoxide: cis-alkene gives cis-epoxide, trans-alkene gives trans-epoxide. No rearrangements occur as mechanism is concerted.

Reaction Conditions

Reagents: Peroxy acids - MCPBA, peroxyacetic acid, peroxybenzoic acid
Alternative: $H_2O_2$ with $WO_3$ catalyst (Sharpless asymmetric epoxidation)
Solvent: $CH_2Cl_2$, $CHCl_3$, or inert solvents
Stereochemistry: Syn addition, stereospecific

Mechanism Steps (Concerted):

Step 1: Peroxy Acid Approach

Peroxy acid approaches alkene in one step. Oxygen transfers from peroxy acid to alkene simultaneously with O-O bond breaking and C-O bond formation.

Step 2: Epoxide Formation

Concerted mechanism forms epoxide and carboxylic acid. No intermediates, so no carbocation rearrangements.

Exceptional Case: Epoxide Ring Opening

Epoxides can be opened by nucleophiles under acidic or basic conditions. Acidic: nucleophile attacks more substituted carbon (Markovnikov). Basic: nucleophile attacks less substituted carbon (sterics).

Learning Mnemonic

"S-S-S" - Syn addition, Stereospecific, Strained ring. "C-R-A" - Concerted mechanism, Retains alkene configuration, Acidic/basic ring opening different.

Detailed MCQ Explanation Example:

Sample MCQ with Detailed Analysis

Question: trans-2-Butene reacts with peroxybenzoic acid. The product is:

Options: A) cis-2,3-Epoxybutane, B) trans-2,3-Epoxybutane, C) 2,3-Dihydroxybutane, D) 2-Butanone

Step-by-step solution:

  1. Epoxidation is stereospecific syn addition
  2. Alkene configuration retained in epoxide
  3. trans-Alkene gives trans-epoxide
  4. Product: trans-2,3-epoxybutane (racemic mixture)
  5. Thus answer is trans-2,3-epoxybutane → Option B

Learning Trick: Epoxidation retains stereochemistry: cis→cis, trans→trans.

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Stereospecific syn addition
  2. No carbocation, no rearrangements
  3. Electron-rich alkenes react faster
  4. Epoxides can be opened by nucleophiles
  5. Sharpless epoxidation gives enantioselective products

Ozonolysis of Alkenes & Alkynes

Ozonolysis Definition:

Cleavage of carbon-carbon double or triple bonds with ozone ($O_3$) followed by reductive or oxidative workup. For alkenes: gives carbonyl compounds (aldehydes and/or ketones). For alkynes: gives carboxylic acids or diketones.

$R-CH=CH-R' \xrightarrow[1. O_3]{2. Zn/H_2O} R-CHO + R'-CHO$ (Two aldehydes if terminal)
Key Concept: Oxidative vs Reductive Workup

Reductive workup: Zn/H₂O or (CH₃)₂S - gives aldehydes and ketones. Oxidative workup: H₂O₂ - aldehydes oxidized to carboxylic acids. Used to determine alkene structure: identify carbonyl products to deduce original alkene.

Reaction Conditions

Step 1: Ozone ($O_3$) in $CH_2Cl_2$ at -78°C (molozonide formation)
Step 2: Workup - reductive: Zn/H₂O, (CH₃)₂S; oxidative: H₂O₂
For alkynes: H₂O workup gives α-diketones or carboxylic acids
Temperature: Low temperature to prevent ozonide decomposition

Mechanism Steps:

Step 1: Ozonide Formation

O₃ adds to alkene forming molozonide (1,2,3-trioxolane) which rearranges to ozonide.

Step 2: Workup

Reductive: Zn reduces ozonide to carbonyl compounds. Oxidative: H₂O₂ oxidizes any aldehydes to carboxylic acids.

Exceptional Case: Ozonolysis of Alkynes

With alkynes: gives α-diketones if internal, or carboxylic acids if terminal. Terminal alkyne: $RC≡CH → RCOOH + HCOOH$ (formic acid oxidizes to CO₂ + H₂O).

Learning Mnemonic

"O-Z-O" - Ozone then Zinc gives Original carbonyls (aldehydes/ketones). "O-P-O" - Ozone then Peroxide Oxidizes aldehydes to acids. "T-A-C" - Terminal Alkynes give Carboxylic acids.

Detailed MCQ Explanation Example:

Sample MCQ with Detailed Analysis

Question: An alkene on ozonolysis gives only acetone. The alkene is:

Options: A) Propene, B) 2-Methylpropene, C) 2,3-Dimethyl-2-butene, D) 2-Methyl-2-butene

Step-by-step solution:

  1. Only acetone (CH₃COCH₃) means both cleavage products are acetone
  2. Alkene must be symmetrical with both carbons identical
  3. Each carbon becomes carbonyl carbon after cleavage
  4. 2,3-Dimethyl-2-butene: (CH₃)₂C=C(CH₃)₂ → 2(CH₃)₂C=O (acetone)
  5. Thus answer is 2,3-dimethyl-2-butene → Option C

Learning Trick: Count carbons: Alkene carbon becomes carbonyl carbon. Symmetrical alkene gives identical carbonyls.

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Reductive workup: Zn/H₂O gives aldehydes and ketones
  2. Oxidative workup: H₂O₂ gives carboxylic acids from aldehydes
  3. Terminal alkene gives formaldehyde (HCHO) or formic acid (HCOOH)
  4. Alkyne ozonolysis: α-diketones or carboxylic acids
  5. Used for structure determination of unknown alkene

Polymerization of Alkenes

Polymerization Definition:

Process where monomer molecules (alkenes) join together to form large polymer chains. Two main types: addition polymerization (chain growth) and condensation polymerization (step growth). Alkenes undergo addition polymerization via radical, cationic, or anionic mechanisms.

$n CH_2=CHX \rightarrow [-CH_2-CHX-]_n$ (Polymer chain)
Key Concept: Chain Growth Mechanism

Addition polymerization has three stages: Initiation (radical/cation/anion formation), Propagation (chain growth), and Termination (chain ends meet). Tacticity: isotactic (all same side), syndiotactic (alternating), atactic (random).

Reaction Conditions

Radical polymerization: Peroxides or AIBN initiator, heat/light
Cationic polymerization: Lewis acids (BF₃, AlCl₃) with co-initiator, low temp
Anionic polymerization: Strong bases (BuLi), monomers with e⁻ withdrawing groups
Ziegler-Natta: TiCl₃/AlEt₃, stereospecific, gives isotactic polymers

Mechanism Steps (Radical Polymerization):

Step 1: Initiation

Peroxide (ROOR) decomposes to radicals (RO•). Radical adds to monomer starting chain.

Step 2: Propagation

Radical adds to another monomer, chain grows. Head-to-tail addition is normal (more stable radical).

Step 3: Termination

Two radicals combine (combination) or disproportionate (H transfer).

Exceptional Case: Ziegler-Natta Polymerization

Coordination polymerization gives stereoregular polymers (isotactic or syndiotactic). Catalyst controls monomer approach. Polypropylene from Ziegler-Natta is isotactic and crystalline, while radical gives atactic amorphous polymer.

Learning Mnemonic

"R-C-P-T" - Radical, Cationic, Polymerization Three steps (Initiation, Propagation, Termination). "Z-N-I" - Ziegler-Natta gives Isotactic polymers. "H-T-N" - Head-to-tail is Normal (most stable intermediate).

Detailed MCQ Explanation Example:

Sample MCQ with Detailed Analysis

Question: Which monomer gives polyvinyl chloride (PVC)?

Options: A) CH₂=CHCl, B) CH₂=CH₂, C) CH₂=CHCH₃, D) CF₂=CF₂

Step-by-step solution:

  1. Polyvinyl chloride: repeating unit -[-CH₂-CHCl-]-
  2. Monomer must have CH₂=CH- unit with Cl substituent
  3. Vinyl chloride: CH₂=CHCl polymerizes to PVC
  4. CH₂=CH₂ gives polyethylene, CH₂=CHCH₃ gives polypropylene
  5. Thus answer is CH₂=CHCl → Option A

Learning Trick: "Poly" + "monomer name" = polymer. Polyvinyl chloride from vinyl chloride, polystyrene from styrene, etc.

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Radical polymerization: peroxides, heat/light
  2. Cationic: for alkenes with e⁻ donating groups (isobutylene)
  3. Anionic: for alkenes with e⁻ withdrawing groups (styrene, acrylonitrile)
  4. Ziegler-Natta: stereoregular polymers from α-olefins
  5. Degree of polymerization = molecular weight of polymer / monomer

Detailed MCQ Explanation Example (Advanced Level):

Sample Advanced MCQ with Detailed Analysis

Question: An alkene C₆H₁₂ on ozonolysis gives only propanal. The alkene is:

Options: A) 1-Hexene, B) 2-Hexene, C) 3-Hexene, D) 2-Methyl-2-pentene

Step-by-step solution:

  1. Only propanal (CH₃CH₂CHO) means both cleavage products identical
  2. Alkene must be symmetrical with 6 carbons
  3. Each half must give propanal (3-carbon aldehyde)
  4. So alkene is 3-hexene: CH₃CH₂CH=CHCH₂CH₃
  5. Ozonolysis: cleavage at double bond gives 2× CH₃CH₂CHO
  6. Thus answer is 3-hexene → Option C

Learning Trick: For ozonolysis giving single product: alkene is symmetrical. Count carbons: product carbons × 2 = alkene carbons (minus 2 for double bond).
